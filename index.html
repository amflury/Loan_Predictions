Why Two Datasets

You Probably noticed that we are showing off two Wildly different datasets. We decided to this mostly because of an early mistake.

We started out knowing we wanted to work with loan data, mostly Housing loan data. So we visited Kaggle and found a couple of options and after some discussion and poor attempt at eyeballing how many loans weren't paid back in one dataset (*cough cough* Spencer *cough*) we decided to go with the larger data set you saw above. We then went to work and created a classification model using random forest modeling. At this point we decided to take a look at the scores you will see below and we were not very impressed. After this disappointment, We decided to pivot and create a model from the other dataset to see if we could do better. Well after to putting together two models we decided to add this portion of the project were we compare and contrast the results of our two models. So in the next section you will see a cautionary tail about picking the right dataset to create accurate models.

Small Datasets Vs. Large Datasets

Below we add evidence to what we have been told about picking datasets for Machine Learning based more on the split between negative and positive outcomes and not necessarily based on the size of the dataset. We also see a great example of how data is labeled can lead to confusion down the line.

Our results in full living color.:

Large Dataset Classification Report

As you can see from the scores our model does a great job of predicting people who have back their loans(negative not fully paid), but when we look at how accurate it is at predicting that someone will not pay back their loans(positive not fully paid(I'm still not entirely sure I have that right)) out scores are very low. This makes sense since the model has around 6000 values that come back negative and only about 1500 values that are positive.

Small Dataset Classification Report

Here we are looking at the smaller dataset and as you can see our scoring has gotten much better most likely because instead of having roughly 18% of our dataset being negative roughly 33% of our dataset covers negative values.
